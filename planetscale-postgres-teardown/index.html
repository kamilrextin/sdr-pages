Postgres Positioning Teardown
Prepared for Sam Lambert
Operator Teardown · April 2026

Three companies sell Postgres. None of them sell it to the same person.

A line-by-line read of Neon, Supabase, and PlanetScale Postgres — homepage, pricing, docs, and the words each one owns in a buyer’s head. All copy pulled directly from live pages. No recommendations, no prescriptions, no pitch. Just what’s on the page right now.

Observation in one sentence per vendor

Neon

Assumes the reader doesn’t care about Vitess or sharding history. Pitches Postgres as if Postgres is the whole conversation.

Supabase

Assumes the reader doesn’t care about Postgres itself. Postgres is an implementation detail inside a “development platform.”

PlanetScale Postgres

Assumes the reader already knows what PlanetScale is. The page reads like a product update to an existing customer, not a first meeting.

The read

Neon sells a product category. Supabase sells a workflow. PlanetScale sells a track record.

Neon’s hero is three words a Postgres buyer already uses: fast, Postgres, serverless. The subhead then adds one qualifier — “AI Engineering era” — and stops. A reader who has never heard of Neon can describe what Neon does after one sentence.

Supabase never asks the reader to care about Postgres on its own. The H1 is about outcome (“Build in a weekend, Scale to millions”). The word “Postgres” only appears in the subhead, in a six-item feature list, where it’s first among equals with Auth, Storage, Edge Functions. Postgres is the engine, not the product.

PlanetScale’s Postgres page opens with “the developer experience PlanetScale is known for.” A cold reader doesn’t know what PlanetScale is known for. The sentence only works for someone who already lived through Vitess, sharding, and the migration from branches-for-MySQL. It reads as insider comms — a product launch to a list that already opted in.

02Paid landing / pricing

How each page handles the Postgres buyer specifically.

Pricing is where category assumptions show up most clearly — because every plan name, every column header, every SKU encodes what the vendor thinks the buyer came for.

Neon · /pricing
Headline
“Neon Pricing Plans”
Subhead
“Neon is a serverless Postgres platform with three plans: Free, Launch, and Scale.”
Plan names
Free · Launch · Scale
Buyer segmentation
“Prototypes, side projects, and small teams” → “Startups and growing teams” → “Production-grade workloads and larger companies”
neon.com/pricing
Supabase · /pricing
Page title
“Pricing & Fees | Supabase”
Structure
Single unified plan ladder (Free → Pro → Team → Enterprise). No Postgres-specific SKU. Database, Auth, Storage, Realtime, Edge Functions all included in the same tier.
What the Postgres buyer sees
No way to buy “just Postgres.” Postgres is metered inside the platform plan alongside Auth MAUs, Storage GB, Edge Function invocations. The pricing page treats the Postgres buyer as the platform buyer.
supabase.com/pricing
PlanetScale · /pricing
Headline
“PlanetScale pricing”
Structure
Two explicitly separate SKU tables: “Postgres SKUs” (EBS HA 3-node, EBS non-HA single node, Metal 3-node) and “Vitess SKUs” (non-metal, metal).
What the Postgres buyer sees
A table that assumes they already know what HA, non-HA, Metal, and a PS-10 x86-64 instance mean. No plan names like Free/Pro/Scale — just compute SKUs keyed on instance size.
planetscale.com/pricing
The read

Neon sells plans. Supabase sells a platform. PlanetScale sells infrastructure.

Neon’s pricing page mentions Postgres in its first sentence, then abstracts away into compute / storage / branches — but the plan names (Free, Launch, Scale) map to team size, not technical depth. A non-expert can self-serve. A database engineer can also self-serve. Both buyers land on the same ladder.

Supabase’s pricing simply doesn’t have a lane for “I only want the database.” If you’re on this page, you’re buying the bundle. That’s coherent with the homepage — but it means a Postgres-first buyer with an existing Auth and Storage stack has nowhere to land.

PlanetScale’s pricing is the most technical of the three and the most legible to someone who already decided. The cost of that legibility: a cold Postgres buyer has to understand EBS vs Metal, HA vs non-HA, and PS-10 vs PS-20 before they can estimate their bill. Pricing pre-qualifies the reader instead of converting them.

03Dev doc entry point

How fast a cold Postgres buyer gets to something useful.

Docs are the second hero. For a technical buyer, the /docs landing is often where the real decision happens — because it’s where they check whether the product survives contact with their stack.

Neon · /docs
H1 / opening line
“Neon is serverless Postgres designed to help you build faster. Autoscaling, branching, instant restore, and more.”
First things a new user sees
“Learn the basics” · “Connect your stack” · “Learn about branching” · “Set up Neon Auth”
Time to a connection string
One command (npx neonctl@latest init) surfaced on the docs home itself. Framework quickstarts (Next.js, Django, Express) are linked from the first screen.
neon.com/docs
Supabase · /docs
H1
“Supabase Documentation”
Subhead
“Learn how to get up and running with Supabase through tutorials, APIs and platform resources.”
Top sections in order
Getting Started · Products · Modules · Client Libraries · Migrate to Supabase
Products shown first
Database · Auth · Storage · Realtime · Edge Functions. No dedicated Postgres-only lane; Postgres lives inside “Database.”
supabase.com/docs
PlanetScale · /docs
H1
“PlanetScale documentation”
Opener
“PlanetScale is a relational database platform that brings you scale, performance, and reliability — without sacrificing developer experience.”
First interaction
Two cards: Vitess documentation and Postgres documentation. The reader is asked to pick a lane before they see a quickstart.
Time to a connection string
Gated by one extra click and one extra decision — plus, further down, a “PlanetScale plans” section and deployment architecture detail before code.
planetscale.com/docs
The read

Neon treats the docs home like a landing page. The other two treat it like a table of contents.

Neon’s docs home repeats the category claim (“serverless Postgres”) and drops a copy-pasteable command on the same screen. A cold buyer lands on /docs and can be typing a real command in under thirty seconds. That’s rare.

Supabase’s docs home is genuinely a table of contents. That’s consistent with a platform positioning — when you sell five products, the docs can’t bias toward one — but it means a Postgres-only buyer has to self-route through “Products → Database” before anything feels specific to them.

PlanetScale’s docs home asks the buyer to make the Vitess-or-Postgres choice up front. For an existing customer that’s a feature. For a cold buyer who hasn’t decided yet, it’s a friction point that every comparison tab they left open doesn’t have.

04Category language

Which words each company owns in the Postgres buyer’s head.

Not what they say they do. What phrase a buyer would actually use to describe them to a colleague twelve hours after leaving the site.

Neon
Serverless Postgres
Two words, on every surface — hero tagline, pricing subhead, docs H1. Category-defining positioning. A buyer who left five minutes ago still remembers “serverless Postgres.” That’s the whole product in two words.
Supabase
The Postgres development platform
Emphasis on platform, not Postgres. Supabase has spent years successfully owning the “open-source Firebase alternative” phrase externally, and the homepage subhead now reinforces the platform framing with a six-item feature list. Postgres is inside the brand, not the brand itself.
PlanetScale
The world’s fastest and most scalable cloud databases
The main homepage frames the category as speed + scale across “both our Postgres and Vitess databases.” The /postgres page narrows to “PlanetScale Postgres” and “PlanetScale Metal.” The buyer leaves remembering “fast” and “Metal” — strong primitives, but not a category phrase a new buyer would reach for when searching.
The read

One vendor owns a category phrase. One owns a platform promise. One owns a performance claim.

“Serverless Postgres” is a search term. “Postgres development platform” is a pitch. “Fastest and most scalable” is a benchmark claim. Each of those is legitimate — but they don’t compete for the same mental shelf.

A buyer typing “serverless Postgres” into a search bar has already pre-qualified themselves for Neon. A buyer typing “Firebase alternative” has already pre-qualified for Supabase. PlanetScale’s current Postgres framing doesn’t yet have an equivalent short phrase that pulls buyers toward it from the search bar — it relies on the buyer already having the brand loaded.
